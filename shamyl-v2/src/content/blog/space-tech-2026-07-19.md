Here's your Sunday space tech roundup for July 19, 2026:

![Space Technology](/images/space-tech-2026-07-19-1.jpg)

## Launches & Missions

- **India's Skyroot Aerospace Reaches Orbit** — In a historic achievement, Skyroot Aerospace's Vikram-1 rocket successfully reached orbit on its maiden flight. This marks India's first private orbital launch, demonstrating the growing capabilities of commercial space in emerging markets.

- **SpaceX Starship Flight 13 Aborted** — SpaceX scrubbed Starship's 13th test flight due to engine ignition issues. While disappointing, the company is known for its rapid iteration approach to development. The next attempt is expected within days.

- **NASA Psyche Mission Mars Flyby** — NASA's Psyche spacecraft captured detailed images of Mars during its gravity assist flyby. The mission, en route to the metal-rich asteroid Psyche, used the planetary encounter to gain velocity while documenting Martian surface features.

## SpaceX & Commercial Space

- **SpaceX Launches 21 Military Satellites** — SpaceX successfully deployed 21 satellites for the military's new data network, demonstrating the Falcon 9's reliability for national security missions. This launch represents the growing role of commercial providers in defense infrastructure.

- **SpaceX's "Only Problem is Space"** — As reported by SpaceNews, SpaceX's primary challenge isn't technology or demand—it's finding enough physical space to build and process rockets. The company's Starbase facility is operating at capacity as launch cadence continues to increase.

- **Crew Dragon Availability Concerns** — Industry officials are expressing worry about Crew Dragon availability as the spacecraft is now the sole U.S. crew transport to the ISS following Boeing Starliner delays. NASA is working to certify additional vehicles while SpaceX scales production.

![Space Exploration](/images/space-tech-2026-07-19-2.jpg)

## Satellites & Constellations

- **Space Force Triples Launch Contracts** — The U.S. Space Force has dramatically increased its launch contract ceiling to meet rising satellite deployment demands. This expansion reflects the accelerating pace of both military and commercial satellite constellations.

- **Pentagon Awards $7.1M for Satellite Cover Glass** — The Department of Defense awarded $7.1 million to expand U.S. production of cover glass for satellites—a critical component for protecting solar panels and sensors from space radiation and debris.

- **DIU Seeks Orbital Power Solutions** — The Defense Innovation Unit is exploring commercial paths to deliver electrical power from orbit, potentially revolutionizing how energy is distributed to remote military operations.

## Deep Space & Exploration

- **NASA Terminates Draper Lunar Lander Mission** — In a significant setback, NASA has canceled the Draper lunar lander mission originally scheduled for CLPS delivery. The decision reflects the agency's risk assessment as it pushes toward sustainable lunar presence.

- **Nuclear Power in Space Milestone** — Miami-based City Labs achieved a first for commercial nuclear power in space, demonstrating compact nuclear batteries that could power long-duration missions where solar isn't viable.

- **NASA May Send Nuclear Mars Rover to Moon** — NASA is considering repurposing a backup nuclear-powered Mars rover design for a lunar mission, leveraging existing technology for rapid lunar surface exploration.

## Research & Discoveries

- **Chandra and IXPE Study Lighthouse Nebula Pulsar** — Combined observations from NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory and IXPE are revealing new details about a pulsar in the Lighthouse Nebula, helping scientists understand extreme physics in collapsed stellar remnants.

- **New Method to Spot Nuclear Weapons in Orbit** — A Nature study has identified innovative techniques to detect nuclear weapons placed in orbit, addressing growing concerns about space-based nuclear capabilities.

- **Artemis Accords Grow to 70 Nations** — Mauritius became the 70th nation to sign the Artemis Accords, while Serbia also joined this week. The international framework for lunar exploration continues to gain global support.

## International Developments

- **Russia's Falcon 9 Competitor** — Questions remain about whether Russia's answer to the Falcon 9 will ever achieve flight status. Development challenges and funding constraints continue to hamper the project.

- **NASA Invites Media to Roman Space Telescope Launch** — Preparations are underway for the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, with media accreditation now open. This next-generation observatory will complement Webb with wide-field infrared surveys.

### Key Takeaway

This week highlights both the triumphs and challenges of modern space exploration. While Skyroot's successful orbital debut shows space democratization in action, setbacks like the Starship abort and Draper lander cancellation remind us that space remains difficult. The increasing integration of commercial capabilities into national security infrastructure—through military satellite launches and the Space Force's expanding contracts—signals a fundamental shift in how space power is projected. As we approach the 70th nation signing the Artemis Accords, international cooperation in space continues to grow, even as new capabilities like nuclear power in orbit and space-based energy distribution point toward humanity's expanding footprint beyond Earth.
